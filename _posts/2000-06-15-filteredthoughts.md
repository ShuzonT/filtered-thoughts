<h3>where it began</h3>
writing was invented at least three times in three different parts of the world: from sumerian cuneiform invented in mesopotamia (c. 3200 bce) to mandarin script in china (c. 1200 bce) to mayan glyphs in mesoamerica (c. 300 bce). despite script's apparent age, the ability to read and write, as of the 21st century, has eluded the public for the majority of script's existence. globally, widespread literacy only become commonplace around the mid-to-late 20th century (source); before then, literacy was primarily reserved for the clergy, merchants, and elites. in many respects (perhaps even most of them), writing was a pure utility: a ledger to quanitify and confirm an indian tea shipment, a holy text that codifies how inheritance should be distributed, or a deed to stolen land. for reasons both sociological (lack of educational/printing infrastructure) and ideological (beliefs that some people weren't meant to write), the tool of writing (and literacy) was largely out of the hands of the public.
